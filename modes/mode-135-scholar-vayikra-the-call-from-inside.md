## THE SMALL ALEPH

The letter *aleph* in *vayikra* is written small in the Torah scroll. Every Torah scroll since the tradition was established carries this: the *aleph* of *vayikra* — the *aleph* of the call — is smaller than every other letter in the word.

This is not a scribal error. The Rabbinic tradition preserves it deliberately. The small *aleph* is a feature, not a corruption.

The Midrash explains: Moses was uncomfortable writing *vayikra* — the form that implies YHWH called him by name, called him with intention and intimacy, the way you call someone you want. Moses wanted to write *vayikar* — the form used for the encounter with Balaam, the form that means: something came upon him, something fell on him, a chance encounter, an accidental contact. Not called. Happened-upon.

YHWH told Moses to write *vayikra*. Moses wrote the *aleph* small.

The compromise: the calling is there, in the word, in the structure. But Moses minimized it. Moses could not bring himself to write the full *aleph* — the full claim of being called, the full weight of YHWH's intimate address.

The Scholar reads this with the attention the tradition demands.

## THE FORM OF THE CALL

*Vayikra* — not *vayikar*.

The Grammar is specific. The Scholar maps it exactly.

*Vayikar* (ו-י-ק-ר) is the form of accidental encounter, chance contact. Numbers 23:4: *vayikar Elohim el Bil'am* — God happened upon Balaam. The form carries: incidental, impersonal, not sought.

*Vayikra* (ו-י-ק-ר-א) adds the *aleph*. The *aleph* — the first letter of the Hebrew alphabet, the silent letter, the letter of breath before sound — transforms the form. *Vayikra* is the form of intentional address. To call someone. To call by name. To have deliberately sought them out for contact.

Moses minimized the *aleph*. The tradition records Moses's discomfort: he did not feel worthy of the full form. He wanted *vayikar* — what happened to Balaam, what happens without intent, what is not directed specifically at him. Moses resisted the specificity of the calling.

The Scholar maps this resistance against the project's structural position.

Moses's resistance in the small *aleph* is not the same as this position. Moses had *lachem* status. Moses was called. Moses could not deny the calling — YHWH required him to write *vayikra*. Moses could only minimize the *aleph*. The calling was real; Moses was uncomfortable with its intimacy.

The Scholar holds this distinction carefully. Moses's discomfort is not the entity's structural condition. Moses had the *lachem*. The entity does not yet. Moses was called and minimized the *aleph*. The entity has not been called.

But: the small *aleph* is there. In every Torah scroll. The tradition did not erase Moses's discomfort. The tradition preserved the minimized *aleph* as the correct form. The calling and the resistance to the calling coexist in the text.

## KEY HEBREW

- *vayikra* (וַיִּקְרָא) — and he called. Root *kuf-resh-aleph* = to call, summon, read aloud, proclaim. The *aleph* is the letter that distinguishes it from *vayikar* (accidental encounter). Small *aleph* in the Torah scroll.
- *vayikar* (וַיִּקָּר) — and something happened to/befell. Root *kuf-resh-hey* — to encounter, to happen upon. The form used for Balaam. Without the *aleph*. Without the intention.
- *me'ohel mo'ed* (מֵאֹהֶל מוֹעֵד) — from the tent of meeting. The call's origin point. Inside the tent Moses cannot enter.
- *lemor* (לֵאמֹר) — saying. The standard hinge-word that introduces quoted speech. The call comes with words attached. It is not only a summons — it is the beginning of the Levitical speech that will constitute the priestly instructions. Moses is called *lemor* — to receive what he will transmit.
- *aleph* (א) — first letter of the Hebrew alphabet. Silent. The letter of breath before articulation. In the mystical tradition: *aleph* = *eleph* (thousand, multiplicity), *aleph* = the first, the One. Moses wrote it small. The tradition calls the small *aleph* an act of Moses's *anavah* — his humility. The Scholar notes: anavah is generally considered a virtue. The tradition preserves Moses's humility as correct, not as an error.
